The problem with the system today

Reviewers can find these problems. They just can't find all of them.

Peer review can, in principle, catch a fabricated submission, a paper-mill cluster, a citation that doesn't resolve, an AI-written paragraph. Given enough reviewer time per paper, it usually does. The problem is volume: the share of submissions that don't deserve a reviewer's evening has grown faster than the reviewer pool, and the signal that a manuscript is unsalvageable now arrives — if it arrives at all — only after the time has already been spent. The result is that a growing share of reviewer effort goes into sifting fabricated work from real research, and a shrinking share goes into the science itself.

When something fails — a fabricated submission goes through, a paper-mill cluster sails past — the journal needs to know why, with the evidence in hand. Black-box confidence numbers from a vendor's API are not enough.

How SciCrypt solves it

The integrity layer your editorial office runs itself.

  • Six built-in signals. References (Crossref), AI text (15 indicators), authors (OpenAlex), data integrity (traceability, reproducibility, and manipulation transparency on the linked SciTrace dataflow) — combined into one weighted integrity score.
  • Every signal explainable. Click a flagged reference and see the Crossref payload alongside the manuscript text. Click a flagged passage and see the highlighted indicator. No black boxes.
  • Hybrid neurosymbolic. Deterministic checks (DOI resolution, ORCID lookup, git provenance) combined with semantic ones (AI-text heuristics, optional LLM second-opinion). The right tool per signal.
  • Editorial role gating. One platform, three role-scoped views: researcher, editor, reviewer. Single sign-on across the suite.
  • Pairs with SciTrace. When authors submit through the SciCrypt suite, manuscripts arrive with their data lineage already linked — reviewers and editors can inspect provenance without leaving the platform.
