Why this exists.

SciCrypt sits at the intersection of three industry shifts: funder mandates for FAIR data, the explosion of paper-mill and AI-generated content, and a reviewer pool whose hours are being absorbed by triage work that has nothing to do with the science. The thesis is simple: SciTrace — the provenance layer researchers depend on — should be open, inspectable, and self-hostable; SciVerify — the editorial layer journals depend on — should give reviewers back the time to focus on the science and the knowledge a paper adds.

FAIR data isn't optional anymore.

The DFG's Guidelines for Safeguarding Good Research Practice require that data underlying published findings be retained "for an appropriate period — generally at least ten years." The EU's FAIR principles ("Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable") align with that direction. The NIH 2023 Data Management and Sharing Policy enforces something similar. A SciTrace dataflow is, by construction, FAIR — git-annex layout, machine-readable metadata, tracked-run provenance commits, integrity score verifiable on demand.

That's why SciTrace is open source. The provenance layer of science is not somewhere to lock the world's data inside a vendor's database. It belongs in the hands of researchers, on their own infrastructure, on tools they can read and fork.

The bottleneck moved. It's not detection anymore — it's triage.

The hard part of integrity work is no longer spotting a bad submission. Peer review, given time, can usually do that. The hard part is doing it at the rate submissions now arrive: separating the manuscripts worth a reviewer's evening from the ones that should never have reached a reviewer at all. Today that triage is performed by the same people whose job is to evaluate the science, and it consumes a share of their time that grows with every wave of fabricated and AI-generated submissions that lands at the desk.

SciVerify's role is to give the editorial office back that triage time. It runs the checks that don't need a human — reference resolution, ORCID linkage, author identity against the literature, provenance of any linked dataflow — and flags what looks off, with the evidence attached. What the reviewer then sees is a manuscript that has already been separated from the obvious rejections, with a structured trail of why it passed. Peer review goes back to being a conversation about the science and the knowledge a paper adds, not a first-pass filter for industrial-scale fabrication.
